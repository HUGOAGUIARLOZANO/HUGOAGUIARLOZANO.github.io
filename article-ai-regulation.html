Artificial Intelligence and the Law: Three Regulatory Models in Collision

Hugo Aguiar · March 28, 2026 · 10 min read · AI & Regulation

The Regulatory Race

The world is simultaneously building the most powerful technology in human history and trying to write rules for it. The results, so far, are three fundamentally different regulatory philosophies — and an emerging problem for every organization, government, and lawyer operating across jurisdictions.

This is not a debate about whether to regulate AI. That debate is over. AI regulation has shifted from policy development to active enforcement across major global markets. The question now is which model wins — or whether the collision between them creates a compliance environment that is manageable by no one.

The EU Model: Rights-First, Risk-Based, Extraterritorial

The EU AI Act entered into force on August 1, 2024, and will be fully applicable on August 2, 2026. It is the world's first comprehensive legal framework on AI — and it was designed to travel.

The Act organizes AI systems into four risk tiers: unacceptable risk (banned), high risk (strictly regulated), limited risk (transparency obligations), and minimal risk (largely unregulated). Applications that create unacceptable risk — such as government-run social scoring — are banned. High-risk applications, such as CV-scanning tools that rank job applicants, are subject to specific legal requirements.

Organizations non-compliant with the EU AI Act face penalties reaching up to 35 million euros or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. Like GDPR, the AI Act applies extraterritorially to any organization deploying AI systems that affect EU users — regardless of where the organization is headquartered.

The US Model: Innovation-First, Jurisdictionally Fragmented

Since Donald Trump began his second term in 2025, federal AI policy has emphasized innovation-first approaches while states continue to pass enforceable AI rules taking effect in 2026. The AI Transparency Act and the Generative AI Training Data Transparency Act both took effect on January 1, 2026 in California, requiring disclosure of AI-generated content, public summaries of training datasets, and controls around detection tools.

President Trump signed an Executive Order in December 2025 to block state-level AI laws incompatible with a minimally burdensome national policy framework. The result is legal uncertainty layered on top of regulatory fragmentation — a compliance environment that penalizes exactly the kind of medium-sized organizations that cannot afford large legal departments in every jurisdiction.

Latin America: The Window Is Open, But Closing

Brazil's Bill No. 2338, approved by the Senate in December 2024 and awaiting final approval, would introduce a comprehensive AI framework closely aligned with the EU AI Act. Chile ranks first in one Latin American AI Index as it expands construction of data centers and promotes local AI startups. Argentina has a pending bill on personal data protection in AI systems. Most other countries in the region have no binding AI-specific legislation yet.

This is the window. The countries that design their AI regulatory frameworks now — before large-scale deployment of high-risk AI systems in public services, judicial decisions, credit allocation, and energy infrastructure — will retain genuine regulatory sovereignty. The countries that wait will find themselves choosing between the EU model and the US model with little capacity to adapt either to their institutional context.

What the Three Models Actually Regulate

Across all three models, the same categories of AI application are consistently identified as requiring heightened scrutiny: employment decisions, credit allocation, healthcare, education, and public services. Korea, Kazakhstan, Vietnam, and Brazil have all passed AI laws using a risk-based classification where these use-cases are subject to strict obligations. The convergence on high-risk categories is not accidental — it reflects a global recognition that AI systems making consequential decisions about people's lives require accountability mechanisms that voluntary governance cannot provide.

For energy sector professionals, the implication is concrete: AI systems used in procurement decisions at state-owned enterprises, credit risk assessments for energy project financing, and algorithmic allocation of grid resources are all within the scope of high-risk classification in every binding framework currently in force.

The Specific Problem for Latin American Institutions

Latin America faces a regulatory problem structurally different from the EU's or the US's. The region's public institutions are increasingly deploying AI systems built on infrastructure and foundation models that originate entirely outside the region. The regulatory frameworks governing those models are being written in Brussels and Washington — and they are extraterritorial by design.

This creates a sovereignty gap. A Latin American government that deploys a US-built AI system for automated decision-making in public procurement is simultaneously subject to the EU AI Act (if any EU-headquartered suppliers are involved), California's transparency laws (if the foundation model was trained there), and whatever domestic framework the country has — which in most cases is none.

Three Things Latin American Regulators Should Do Now

First, map the AI systems already deployed in public institutions before designing new legislation. The gap between what policymakers assume is being used and what is actually operating in procurement, justice, and social services is consistently larger than expected.

Second, adopt EU-aligned risk classification as the baseline framework. The EU AI Act is the most technically developed regulatory architecture available, and Brazil's decision to align with it is strategically correct. Countries that adopt incompatible frameworks will face barriers in cross-border AI partnerships and procurement with EU-based suppliers.

Third, regulate the contractual architecture of AI procurement before regulating the technology itself. The terms of cloud service agreements, data retention clauses, model audit rights, and liability allocation between vendor and deployer are where regulatory leverage is highest and where current practice is most inadequate.

The Structural Conclusion

AI legal risk now spans privacy, employment, IP, cybersecurity, antitrust, and litigation. That fragmentation is not a temporary condition of an immature regulatory field — it is the permanent architecture of a technology that touches every domain of law simultaneously.

The lawyers and policy professionals who will be most valuable in this environment are not those who specialize narrowly in AI law. They are the ones who can map the intersection of AI governance with the specific legal domains that govern their clients' most consequential decisions: energy contracts, public procurement, financial regulation, and labor law. In Latin America, that work is happening largely without the institutional infrastructure to manage it.
