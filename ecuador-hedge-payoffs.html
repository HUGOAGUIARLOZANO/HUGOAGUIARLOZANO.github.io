Four Structures, One Problem: The Payoff Mathematics of Ecuador's Sovereign Oil Hedge

Why the choice between an Asian put, a collar, and a three-way collar is not a matter of preference — it is a matter of what each structure does to Ecuador's fiscal revenue distribution under dollarization constraints.

By Hugo Aguiar Lozano  ·  LL.M. Harvard Law School (Dean's Scholar)  ·  Former Minister of Energy, Ecuador  ·  Former GM, EP Petroecuador  ·  April 2026

Every sovereign oil hedging decision is, at its core, a decision about which part of the price distribution to sacrifice and which to protect. An unhedged position is full exposure: gains unlimited, losses unlimited, fiscal revenue tracking oil prices with no intervention. A put option buys downside protection at the cost of a known premium, leaving upside intact. A collar sells some upside to finance that protection. A three-way collar goes further, selling a second tranche of downside protection to reduce premium costs. Each structure implies a specific payoff function, a specific fiscal revenue profile, and a specific set of political economy risks that no spreadsheet comparison of three scenarios fully captures.

This paper makes the payoff mathematics of each structure explicit and interactive, grounding them in Ecuador's 2026 fiscal context: approximately 120 million barrels annually hedged, a fiscal sensitivity of roughly $23.85 million per $1/barrel per the IMF EFF program adjuster,1 and a BCE regulatory framework (Resolution JPRFM-2026-009-M) that is operationally compatible with some structures and institutionally hostile to others.2 The analysis revisits the benchmark cost-efficiency estimate of Satyanarayan and Somensatto (1997) — who found that 1% of risk reduction cost 0.65% of expected return under 1988–1996 conditions3 — and updates it to current implied volatility (σ ≈ 35%), where that ratio has deteriorated to approximately 1.0–1.2 for standard Asian puts.

I. The Mathematical Foundation

1.1 The Mean-Variance Framework

The foundational hedging model for Ecuador was developed by Satyanarayan and Somensatto in World Bank Policy Research Working Paper No. 1792. They model the hedging decision as a portfolio selection problem where the government minimizes revenue variance subject to a return constraint. For a risk-minimizing short hedger, the optimal hedge ratio is:

(1) h* = Cov(ΔS, ΔF) / Var(ΔF)

The portfolio return and variance are:

(2–3) ERₚ = E(ΔS) − h · E(ΔF) Varₚ = Var(S) + h² · Var(F) − 2h · Cov(S,F)

Their key empirical findings for Ecuador over 1988–1996: optimal hedge ratio h* ≈ 0.89, basis risk (1 − R²) = 0.19, and a cost elasticity at the minimum-variance portfolio of 0.65 — meaning a 1% reduction in revenue variance cost 0.65% of expected return.

1.2 The Four Payoff Functions

For an Asian put option — the appropriate instrument for Ecuador's annual fiscal cycle — the payoff at settlement is:

(4) Payoff(put) = max(K − S̄, 0) × V₀ − P × V₀ where S̄ = (1/T) Σ Sᵢ [arithmetic average over T monthly fixings] K = strike price (USD/bbl) V₀ = hedged volume (barrels) P = option premium (USD/bbl)

For the collar, Ecuador buys a put at K₁ and sells a call at K₂ > K₁:

(5) Payoff(collar) = [max(K₁ − S̄, 0) − max(S̄ − K₂, 0)] × V₀ − Pⁿᵉᵗ × V₀

For the three-way collar, a short put at K₃ < K₁ is added:

(6) Payoff(3-way) = Payoff(collar) + max(K₃ − S̄, 0) × V₀ CRITICAL: When S̄ < K₃, the last term is POSITIVE — Ecuador OWES payments to counterparties. Protection is not only lost but REVERSED.

1.3 The Volatility Premium Problem

Option premiums scale approximately with implied volatility. When σ rises from the 1997 long-run mean of ~21% to current ~35%, premiums increase by a factor of 35/21 ≈ 1.67. For Asian options, the effective volatility is reduced by the averaging mechanism: σᵉᵉᵉ ≈ σ/√3, lowering premiums ~30–40% relative to European puts. The updated cost elasticity is:

(7) Cost Elasticity ≈ (Premium/Notional) / (Risk Reduction %) At σ = 21% (1997 benchmark): Elasticity ≈ 0.65 At σ = 35% (2026 conditions): Elasticity ≈ 1.0–1.2

The three-way collar converts Ecuador from a protection buyer into a protection seller precisely below the lower strike — at the worst possible moment for a dollarized economy with no exchange rate buffer.

III. What the Diagrams Reveal

3.1 The Unhedged Line

The unhedged payoff is a straight line: slope equals total export volume in millions of barrels, intercept zero. At $65/barrel Ecuador mix, 240 million barrels generates $15.6 billion. At $45/barrel, that is $10.8 billion — a $4.8 billion shortfall relative to the baseline. The IMF EFF adjuster quantifies the program-relevant portion: at $23.85 million per dollar/barrel, a $20/barrel decline from the program baseline generates approximately $477 million in fiscal exposure relative to program targets. The unhedged line's symmetry is its defining feature — every hedging instrument breaks that symmetry, and the decision is always about how and at what cost.

3.2 The Asian Put: The Kink at K₁

The put's payoff has a single kink at the strike price K₁. Below the strike, the payoff line flattens: the hedge payout compensates for revenues below the floor, and the effective slope becomes zero (fully protected) for the hedged portion. Above the strike, the line is parallel to the unhedged line but shifted down by the premium cost. This is the geometry of asymmetric protection — losses bounded, gains preserved, cost fixed and known. At $8/barrel on 120 million hedged barrels, the premium is $960 million. This is the number policymakers and legislators will scrutinize when prices rise and the option expires worthless.

3.3 The Collar: Two Kinks, Bounded Range

The collar adds a second kink at K₂. Between K₁ and K₂ — the participation zone — Ecuador's net revenue tracks spot prices exactly. Below K₁, revenues are floored. Above K₂, revenues are capped: Ecuador has sold the right to participate above K₂ to the call buyer. When prices spike to $95/barrel and K₂ = $85, Ecuador forgoes $10 × 120 million = $1.2 billion in revenues. This opportunity cost does not appear in standard budget accounts but is economically real and politically visible the moment it materializes.

3.4 The Three-Way Collar: The Dangerous Third Kink

The three-way collar introduces a third kink at K₃. Between K₃ and K₁, the long put pays out normally. But below K₃, the short put position means Ecuador is paying the counterparty rather than collecting — the payoff line's slope turns negative relative to the standard collar. At current σ = 35% and a starting price of $70/barrel, the probability of the annual average WTI price falling below $50/barrel is approximately 8–12% under log-normal dynamics. This is not a remote tail event — it is a scenario that materialized in 2015–16 and 2020. For a dollarized economy with no exchange rate buffer, experiencing the worst fiscal outcome under the scenario that demands the most fiscal support is not an acceptable risk profile.

Structural Finding

Below K₃, the three-way collar converts Ecuador from a protection buyer into a protection seller. The BCE's DVP framework (which requires full pre-funding before BCE instructs any transaction) is structurally incompatible with this profile: margin calls triggered by price movements below K₃ would require the state enterprise to post additional collateral at exactly the moment of maximum fiscal stress. This is an institutional argument against three-way collars that is independent of any probability calculation.

V. The Optimal Structure

Strike calibration. The put strike should be anchored to the IMF EFF program adjuster price rather than a round number. For 2026, this implies K₁ in the range of $60–62/barrel Ecuador mix, or approximately $65–67/barrel in WTI terms after the spread adjustment. This creates a direct institutional link between the hedge design and the fiscal metric that IMF reviewers, legislators, and markets share.

Coverage ratio from premium budget. The coverage ratio should be determined by a maximum affordable annual premium budget (2–4% of projected oil revenues ≈ $150–380 million), not by a target variance reduction. At $5–8/barrel, this implies coverage of 50–70% of export volumes. Full coverage at current premiums would cost $1.2–1.9 billion annually — unsustainable.

Asian structure exclusively. Settlement based on arithmetic average of monthly prices over the fiscal year, aligned with how the EFF adjuster is calculated. European put options (terminal spot price) create settlement mismatches with actual fiscal impact.

No sold option legs. The BCE framework is legally incompatible with instruments generating variable margin calls. This institutional constraint, correctly understood, is a policy advantage: it rules out the structures that have generated political crises in other sovereign hedging programs.

IFI intermediation. Ecuador should approach the World Bank's commodity risk management facility — which executed Uruguay's 2016 Asian call option program4 — rather than attempting direct market execution. Competitive pricing, technical assistance, and reputational cover are all available through this channel without requiring Ecuador to build market infrastructure it does not have.

Summary Design Recommendation

Optimal structure: Asian put, K₁ ≈ $62/bbl (Ecuador mix), covering 50–60% of annual export volumes, maximum annual premium budget $200–400 million, executed through the World Bank commodity risk management facility, pre-funded through a dedicated premium reserve account consistent with BCE's DVP requirement. This is not the cheapest structure — but it is the only one simultaneously compatible with Ecuador's institutional constraints, fiscal governance requirements, and the political economy of transparency under IMF program review.
