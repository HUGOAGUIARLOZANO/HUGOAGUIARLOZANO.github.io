Governing the Energy Transition: Institutional Challenges in Latin America

Hugo Aguiar · March 15, 2026 · 12 min read · Energy Governance

The Dual Mandate

Latin American governments face a structural tension that no rhetorical commitment to the energy transition can resolve: they depend on hydrocarbon revenues to fund health systems, education, and social protection, while simultaneously facing international pressure — and domestic necessity — to restructure their energy sectors around renewables. This is not a political choice between two options. It is a governance problem that will be resolved, well or poorly, through institutional design.

The region's dependence on extractive revenues is not incidental. In Ecuador, petroleum revenues have historically represented between 30 and 50 percent of the central government's non-financial public sector budget. In Bolivia, natural gas exports have financed the social programs that defined the political economy of the past two decades. In Venezuela, the collapse of PDVSA was not merely an energy sector failure — it was a fiscal collapse that destroyed the state's capacity to deliver basic services. The energy transition in Latin America is, at its core, a fiscal transition. Treating it as anything less is analytically insufficient.

What Institutions Actually Do

The governance literature on energy transitions has tended to focus on regulatory frameworks — carbon pricing mechanisms, renewable portfolio standards, grid integration rules — as if the binding constraint were the absence of the right policy instrument. In Latin America, the binding constraint is institutional capacity: the ability of state agencies to design contracts that resist capture, enforce compliance without political interference, and allocate risk between public and private actors in ways that attract investment without surrendering sovereignty.

Three institutional failures recur across the region with enough consistency to constitute a pattern.

The first is the regulatory capture of energy agencies. In countries where the same ministry that owns the national oil company also regulates it, the regulator's independence is structurally compromised. Ecuador's experience with the relationship between the Ministry of Energy, ARCH (the hydrocarbon regulator), and EP Petroecuador illustrates how this tripartite structure creates accountability gaps that private operators systematically exploit. The regulator cannot credibly sanction an entity that its own ministry controls.

The second is contract opacity. Across the region, the terms of hydrocarbon contracts — including the pricing formulas, logistical arrangements, and quality specifications that determine how much revenue actually reaches the treasury — remain outside the scope of meaningful public audit. The result is what this author has elsewhere called the rent capture gap: the systematic difference between what sovereign resources could generate at international market prices and what actually reaches the fiscal accounts. In Ecuador, this gap has been quantified in official documentation at USD 269 million in a single year.

The third is the mismatch between transition timelines and political cycles. Energy infrastructure investment requires planning horizons of fifteen to thirty years. Democratic political cycles run four to five years. In countries with weak institutional continuity — where incoming administrations routinely reverse the contractual commitments of their predecessors — private investors apply a sovereign risk premium that effectively taxes every renewable energy project before construction begins.

The Renewable Opportunity and Its Limits

Latin America has genuine comparative advantages in renewable energy that are underutilized to a degree that is difficult to justify on purely technical grounds. Brazil's hydropower system remains the most sophisticated in the Western Hemisphere. Chile's Atacama Desert offers solar irradiation levels among the highest on the planet. Colombia's wind resources in La Guajira are exceptional by global standards. Ecuador itself has untapped geothermal potential from its volcanic geography that has been discussed for decades without meaningful development.

The gap between resource endowment and installed capacity is an institutional gap. The Dominican Republic's experience with renewable energy auctions in the 2010s demonstrated that well-designed competitive procurement processes can attract serious private investment even in small markets with limited credit ratings — but only when the regulatory framework is credible enough that investors believe the awarded contracts will be honored across changes of government.

Chile's experience is the most instructive in the region. The combination of a genuinely independent energy regulator, a competitive auction system for long-term power purchase agreements, and a transmission planning process insulated from short-term political pressure produced a renewable energy buildout that has made Chile one of the global leaders in solar penetration relative to grid size. The lesson is not that Chile found the right policy instrument — it is that Chile built the institutional infrastructure to make the policy instrument credible.

The Hydrocarbon Legacy Problem

The transition creates a specific governance challenge that is rarely discussed with sufficient precision: what happens to the institutional apparatus built around hydrocarbon extraction as production declines?

National oil companies represent decades of accumulated technical capacity, regulatory expertise, and — in the best cases — genuine institutional knowledge about contract negotiation, reservoir management, and fiscal optimization. In the worst cases, they represent entrenched rent-seeking coalitions with strong incentives to delay transition policies that would reduce their political relevance.

The governance question is not whether to transition, but how to manage the institutional legacy of the hydrocarbon sector in ways that preserve what is genuinely valuable — technical capacity, negotiating experience, knowledge of international commodity markets — while dismantling the rent-seeking structures that have captured public resources for private benefit.

Ecuador's EP Petroecuador faces this challenge acutely. The institutional reforms initiated between 2021 and 2023 — including changes to procurement benchmark methodology and pricing window design — represent exactly the kind of incremental contractual reform that reduces rent capture without requiring legislative action. The 2026 diesel tender incorporates several of these reforms. Whether they represent structural change or temporary improvement will depend on whether the institutional mechanisms that produced them survive the next change of government.

Toward an Institutional Agenda

Three priorities stand out as both technically tractable and politically feasible within the existing institutional constraints of the region.

The first is independent contract audit. Establishing technically capable, politically insulated audit units within supreme audit institutions — on the model of the U.K. National Audit Office or the Chilean Contraloría — with specific mandates to review hydrocarbon and energy contracts against market benchmarks would close the information asymmetry that currently enables rent capture. This requires neither legislative reform nor new agencies — it requires redirecting existing audit capacity toward the contracts where the largest fiscal flows are at stake.

The second is mandatory disclosure of contract terms. The movement toward beneficial ownership registries in the extractive sector — driven partly by EITI membership requirements — has demonstrated that disclosure mandates, when enforced, change the behavior of both state agencies and private counterparties. Extending mandatory disclosure to the pricing formulas, logistical arrangements, and quality specifications of large hydrocarbon supply contracts would reduce the information advantage that international traders currently exploit.

The third is multi-administration contractual frameworks. Designing renewable energy contracts and hydrocarbon production agreements with stabilization clauses that are legally binding across changes of government — and making those clauses subject to international arbitration — is the most effective mechanism currently available for reducing the sovereign risk premium that inflates the cost of energy transition financing in the region.

Conclusion

The energy transition in Latin America will not be determined by the ambition of national energy plans or the strength of international climate commitments. It will be determined by the quality of the institutions that design, award, monitor, and enforce the contracts through which public resources are mobilized and private investment is attracted.

The region has the resource endowment to be a global leader in both hydrocarbon revenue optimization and renewable energy development. What it lacks — in varying degrees across different countries — is the institutional infrastructure to capture that potential without losing it to rent-seeking, contract opacity, and political interference.

Building that infrastructure is not a technical problem. It is a political economy problem. And it is the central governance challenge of the next decade in Latin American energy policy.
