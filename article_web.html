Punitive Damages, Disgorgement & Unjust Enrichment: Drawing the Line

A global practitioner's guide to the distinctions that matter — and why getting them wrong generates systematic distortions in compensation, deterrence, and due process.

Author Hugo Fernando Aguiar Lozano, Attorney-at-Law
Scope 2010 – 2026 comparative update
Jurisdictions USA · EU · Italy · Germany · Mexico · Brazil · Switzerland

The Central Argument

Three remedial questions — What harm did the victim suffer? What profit did the wrongdoer make? How reprehensible was the conduct? — demand three legally distinct answers. Conflating them produces predictable pathologies: overcompensation, incentivised litigation, and, paradoxically, new instances of unjust enrichment.

This article defends a thesis first advanced in a 2010 comparative study and substantially confirmed by the 2010–2026 legislative and jurisprudential record: punitive damages and unjust enrichment belong to different normative universes. The former seeks to punish and deter through a private sanction; the latter seeks to restore patrimonial equity by eliminating a causeless benefit. Between them lies a third instrument — disgorgement, or gain-based restitution — which operates on a different metric still.

The period 2010–2026 has not invalidated this distinction. It has, however, made it considerably more consequential. Punitive-type mechanisms are no longer confined to common law systems; they have migrated — cautiously, conditionally, and with varying degrees of formalism — into civil law jurisdictions ranging from Italy to Mexico to Brazil. Meanwhile, in the United States, the Supreme Court has spent a decade attempting to prevent disgorgement from silently becoming a punitive sanction. And across the European Union, legislators are actively designing remedy architectures that keep compensation, restitution, and punishment in separate lanes.

The question for 2026 is no longer whether civil law systems can accommodate punitive-type mechanisms. The question is how they can do so without conflating punishment, restitution, and compensation into a single, legally incoherent instrument.

This article maps the territory, analyses the key decisions, and proposes five practical design rules for legislators, judges, and counsel operating across multiple jurisdictions.

Conceptual Framework

Three Distinct Legal Universes

The most consequential source of analytical error in this area is the habit of treating "punitive damages," "disgorgement," and "compensatory indemnification" as points on a single spectrum — differing in degree rather than in kind. They are not. They differ in metric, in justification, and in the legal standards that govern their application.

I
Compensatory Damages

Restore the Victim

Measures and addresses the loss suffered by the injured party. The objective is to return the claimant to the position they occupied before the harm. Overcompensation is itself a wrong: it generates unjust enrichment in the victim's favour.

Governing principle: Restitutio in integrum. The harm is the ceiling.

Metric: plaintiff's loss
II
Disgorgement / Gain-Based Restitution

Strip the Wrongdoer's Gain

Removes the net profit attributable to the wrong from the defendant's estate. The victim's loss is irrelevant to quantum. The remedy is equitable in origin and restorative in character, not penal — unless courts allow it to expand beyond net profits.

Governing principle: Nemo ex delicto lucretur. Net profits are the ceiling.

Metric: wrongdoer's net gain
III
Punitive Damages

Punish and Deter

Sanctions particularly egregious conduct with an award calibrated to the gravity of the wrong and the need for deterrence — not to the plaintiff's loss or the defendant's profit. A private penalty. Its legitimacy depends on the rule of law: legal basis, foreseeability, proportionality, quantitative caps.

Governing principle: Deterrence & retribution. No ceiling except proportionality.

Metric: gravity of conduct
The Practitioner's Test

Before characterising any award, ask: which metric governs quantum? If the answer is the victim's loss → compensation. If it is the wrongdoer's profit → disgorgement/restitution. If it is the gravity of the conduct and deterrence → punitive damages. Where the answer is ambiguous, you have a hybrid instrument that requires explicit statutory authorisation and proportionality review.

The conflation risk is highest at the intersection of disgorgement and punitive damages — the zone the Italian Corte di Cassazione in 2017 called "the grey area between restitution and punishment." When disgorgement is unlimited, extended beyond net profits, or not directed to identifiable victims, it ceases to function as a restitutionary remedy and begins to operate as a punitive sanction without the procedural safeguards that the rule of law demands.

Global Comparative Record

Sixteen Years of Landmark Decisions (2010–2026)

The following timeline traces the most structurally significant decisions and legislative developments shaping the relationship between punitive damages, disgorgement, and unjust enrichment across the world's leading jurisdictions.

2011
United States
Restatement (Third) of Restitution and Unjust Enrichment — ALI

The American Law Institute's landmark codification of unjust enrichment principles provides the most authoritative US framework for distinguishing gain-based remedies from punitive awards, influencing subsequent Supreme Court reasoning in Liu (2020).

2014
Mexico
Amparo Directo 30/2013 — Primera Sala, Suprema Corte de Justicia de la Nación

Mexico's Supreme Court held that moral damage awards may incorporate a dissuasive/punitive dimension in their quantification. A constitutional pivot: civil liability is no longer conceived as purely compensatory. The most important Latin American precedent of the decade.

2014
European Union
Directive 2014/104/EU — Antitrust Damages

The EU explicitly excludes punitive and multiple damages from private antitrust enforcement, enshrining "full compensation without overcompensation" as a foundational European principle. The most direct legislative statement of the civil law orthodoxy on the prohibition of punitive private remedies.

2017
United States
Kokesh v. SEC, 581 U.S. 455 (2017)

The Supreme Court unanimously held that SEC disgorgement constitutes a "penalty" for limitations purposes. The key signal: when a remedy operates without reference to identifiable victims and beyond net profits, it begins to function punitively — with the legal consequences that entails.

2017
Italy
Cass. Sezioni Unite n. 16601/2017

The most significant civil law ruling on punitive damages in the decade. Italy's highest court held that punitive damages are not inherently incompatible with Italian public order — but set strict conditions: a specific statutory legal basis, foreseeability, quantitative caps, and proportionality. The judgment opened a carefully gated door.

2019
Switzerland
Bundesgericht 4A_663/2018

The Swiss Federal Tribunal addressed public policy objections to the recognition of foreign judgments with punitive damages components, maintaining cautious restraint and signalling that disproportionate punitive awards remain incompatible with Swiss public order.

2020
Germany
BGH VI ZR 252/19 — Dieselgate

Germany's Federal Court of Justice resolved mass harm arising from Volkswagen's emissions fraud within a classical compensatory framework under § 826 BGB (intentional damage contrary to public policy): price restitution minus a use-benefit deduction. A paradigm case for producing deterrence-equivalent outcomes without a punitive private remedy.

2020
United States
Liu v. SEC, 591 U.S. ___ (2020)

The Supreme Court permitted SEC disgorgement to continue as equitable relief, but only if limited to net profits and directed to harmed investors. A deliberate effort to "re-equitise" the remedy and prevent it from functioning as a disguised penalty — directly addressing the Kokesh tension.

2020
European Union
Directive 2020/1828 — Representative Actions

The EU's class action directive recommends safeguards against punitive damages in consumer representative actions. The legislative logic: collective mechanisms should facilitate access to justice and full redress — not incentivise "rent-seeking" litigation through punitive multipliers.

2021
United States
NDAA FY2021 § 6501 — Statutory Disgorgement

Congress codified SEC disgorgement authority, expressly linking it to "unjust enrichment" and extending limitation periods. The legislator's move immediately re-ignited debate: has Congress authorised disgorgement as a restitutionary remedy, or as a punitive sanction with a restitutionary label?

2023
European Union
CJEU C-300/21 — Österreichische Post (GDPR Damages)

The Court of Justice clarified that a mere GDPR infringement does not automatically confer a right to compensation; actual damage must be proven. A critical guardrail: preventing GDPR compensation from functioning as a punitive fine. Reinforces the architecture of separating public sanctions (administrative fines) from private compensation.

2024
European Union
Regulation 2024/1689 — AI Act

The EU's landmark AI regulation centres deterrence on ex ante compliance obligations and graduated administrative sanctions — not on private punitive remedies. The design choice reflects a mature European position: deterrence is best achieved through public enforcement, not through private litigation.

2025
Italy
Cass. sez. I n. 31244/2025 — ECLI:IT:CASS:2025:31244CIV

Deepening the 2017 framework, Italy's Court of Cassation applied its conditioned-recognition doctrine to foreign judgments with treble/punitive components, reinforcing the requirements of legality, foreseeability, and proportionality. Italy's position is now the most developed civil law framework for engaging with punitive-type awards.

2026
United States
Sripetch v. SEC — SCOTUS (cert. granted Jan. 2026)

The Supreme Court has accepted a case asking whether disgorgement requires proof of pecuniary harm to identifiable investors. The Court's answer will determine whether disgorgement is a true restitutionary remedy (requiring a victim) or a penalty that can be imposed without one — the definitional question at the heart of the restitution/punishment boundary.

Deep Dives

Four Cases That Reshape the Field

The following cases are not merely interesting precedents — they are structural landmarks that redefine the relationship between compensation, disgorgement, and punishment in their respective legal orders.

Liu v. Securities and Exchange Commission
591 U.S. ___ (2020) · United States Supreme Court
Restitution/Punishment Boundary

Following Kokesh's characterisation of SEC disgorgement as a "penalty" for limitations purposes, the Court faced a starker choice: abolish disgorgement entirely, or articulate the conditions under which it may survive as a legitimate equitable remedy. It chose the latter — but at a price.

Justice Sotomayor's opinion for an 8-1 majority held that courts may order disgorgement of "net profits" as equitable relief, but conditioned this on two requirements: (1) the award must be limited to the defendant's net profits (deducting legitimate business expenses), and (2) the funds must be returned to harmed investors, not deposited in the U.S. Treasury.

Why it matters for comparative law: Liu is the U.S. Supreme Court's most precise articulation of what distinguishes restitution from punishment. The requirements of "net profits" and "victim-orientation" map almost exactly onto the conditions that EU law uses to define acceptable gain-based remedies. The case thus provides a usable analytical framework for jurisdictions drafting disgorgement provisions.

Corte di Cassazione, Sezioni Unite, n. 16601
5 July 2017 · Italy
Civil Law / Punitive Compatibility

For decades, the received view in Italian private law — consistent with the majority position across continental Europe — was that punitive damages were categorically incompatible with the compensatory function of civil liability and therefore contrary to public policy (ordine pubblico). The 2017 Grand Chamber judgment fundamentally revised this position.

The court held that punitive damages are not ontologically incompatible with Italian public order. Crucially, however, the court did not simply open the door: it constructed a careful conditional framework. Recognition of foreign punitive awards requires: (i) a specific legal basis in the source jurisdiction — punitive damages cannot rest on judicial discretion alone; (ii) foreseeability for the defendant at the time of the conduct; (iii) quantitative limits proportionate to the compensatory component; and (iv) overall proportionality of the award.

The 2025 follow-up: Cass. n. 31244/2025 extended this framework to treble damage awards, reinforcing that the critical variables are legality, foreseeability, and proportionality — not the label of the remedy.

For practitioners: The Italian framework now offers the most worked-out civil law test for evaluating whether a punitive or multiplied award from a foreign jurisdiction will be recognised. It is increasingly cited by courts in Spain, Portugal, and Latin American jurisdictions as a model for engagement with punitive damages.

Amparo Directo 30/2013
26 February 2014 · Primera Sala, Suprema Corte de Justicia de la Nación, Mexico
Latin America / Constitutional Tort Law

Mexico's Supreme Court decided a personal injury case arising from negligent hospitality services. The First Chamber's judgment went considerably beyond the facts to articulate a structural reconception of moral damage in Mexican law.

The court held that the quantification of moral damage must account not only for the victim's subjective suffering but also for a dissuasive/preventive dimension — effectively integrating what the common law tradition calls a punitive function into the civil liability framework. The court provided a list of quantification parameters including the defendant's financial capacity, the social impact of the conduct, and the need for deterrence.

Doctrinal significance: This decision represents the most complete Latin American example of "constitutionalisation" of the punitive function in private law — the idea that constitutional rights protection demands that civil liability be capable of serving a deterrent role, not merely a compensatory one. It anticipates, under a constitutional rights register, much of what Argentina's Art. 1714 CCC attempted to achieve through codification.

Caution: The judgment does not create a standalone punitive damages remedy. The punitive dimension is embedded within moral damage — which means it remains subject to the limitations and procedural rules governing that category. This hybrid structure both broadens the remedy and limits its doctrinal clarity.

BGH VI ZR 252/19 — Volkswagen Emissions Fraud
25 May 2020 · Bundesgerichtshof, Germany
Mass Harm / Compensatory Framework

The Volkswagen "Dieselgate" scandal produced one of the most instructive comparative law experiments of the decade. The same fundamental wrong — systematic, deliberate fraud affecting millions of consumers — was addressed through entirely different remedial architectures on either side of the Atlantic.

In the United States, the remedy was a negotiated settlement combining a USD 14.7 billion consumer buyback/compensation programme, civil penalties paid to the U.S. government, and criminal fines — a complex public-private architecture. In Germany, the Federal Court of Justice resolved the private law claims under § 826 BGB (intentional damage contrary to good morals), awarding the price paid minus a use-benefit deduction. No punitive element, no disgorgement of profits — pure compensation, calibrated to the individual purchaser's loss.

What the comparison reveals: The German approach demonstrates that a classical civil law framework can generate significant deterrence-equivalent outcomes through the combination of compensatory civil awards at scale, regulatory sanctions, and criminal prosecution — without a punitive private damages remedy. The architecture distributes the sanctioning function across public and private law rather than concentrating it in a single private award. Whether this is preferable to the U.S. model depends on which values one prioritises: certainty of compensation, severity of deterrence, or the distribution of enforcement costs between public agencies and private litigants.

Emerging Frontier

Unjust Enrichment in the Digital Economy

The most intellectually fertile frontier in contemporary unjust enrichment scholarship is also, practically, among the most consequential: the unauthorised commercial use of data — and in particular, the use of copyrighted works and personal data to train generative AI systems.

The doctrinal appeal of unjust enrichment in this context is straightforward. Where a company uses protected works or personal data without authorisation, and derives substantial commercial value from that use, the unjust enrichment framework offers a potentially powerful remedy — one that focuses not on the (often difficult to prove) harm suffered by the data subject, but on the gain made by the defendant.

Unjust enrichment may prove to be the most effective private law remedy for AI-driven data appropriation — precisely because it measures the defendant's gain, not the plaintiff's loss.

Li & Lee, "Unjust Enrichment as a Remedy for AI's Unauthorised Use of Protected Data," Common Law World Review (2026)

The EU's approach, as crystallised in the GDPR litigation and the AI Act, reflects a deliberate architectural choice: keep private compensation distinct from public sanction. The CJEU's Österreichische Post ruling (C-300/21, 2023) exemplifies this: actual damage must be proven for a compensation claim; the mere fact of an infringement does not suffice. This prevents GDPR compensation from functioning as a multiplied administrative fine in the hands of private litigants.

The unresolved question — now beginning to be addressed in scholarship — is whether gain-based restitution (unjust enrichment) offers a bridge between these two positions: allowing private litigants to recover the value extracted without converting compensation into a punitive instrument. The answer will depend on whether courts treat the "benefit" as the entire commercial value of the AI model (very large, raising punitive concerns) or as a traceable net enrichment attributable to the specific unauthorised use (more modest, more defensible as restitution).

Practitioner Note: AI Disputes

Counsel advising AI companies on data acquisition risk should evaluate potential unjust enrichment exposure alongside copyright and privacy claims. Unlike damages, unjust enrichment does not require proof of loss — it requires proof that the defendant was enriched, that the enrichment was at the claimant's expense, and that there is no sufficient legal basis for it. In jurisdictions that accept gain-based restitution, this is a potentially material exposure that goes beyond the quantifiable harm-based claims.

Policy & Practice

Five Design Rules for Legislators, Judges & Counsel

The comparative analysis 2010–2026 generates five operational rules — applicable to legislators drafting remedial frameworks, to courts applying or recognising punitive-type awards, and to counsel advising clients in cross-border disputes.

01

Require a Statutory Basis for Any Punitive Component

Punitive awards — whether labelled "punitive damages," "dissuasive sanctions," or "exemplary damages" — must rest on an explicit legal basis. Judicial discretion is insufficient. The Italian framework (legality, foreseeability, quantitative limits) represents the minimum standard consistent with the rule of law.

02

Use Distinct Metrics for Each Remedy

In any complex dispute involving compensation, disgorgement, and punitive awards simultaneously, ensure that each remedy is quantified on a distinct metric: loss (compensation), net profits (disgorgement), gravity and deterrence (punitive). When a single figure must serve all three purposes, it will fail at all three.

03

Design the Destination of Any "Surplus"

Where an award exceeds the victim's actual loss — whether through a punitive component or a statutory multiplier — the destination of the surplus requires deliberate design. Options include: (a) returning to the victim (risk: unjust enrichment of claimant); (b) payment to a public fund (character: public fine); (c) sector-specific remediation fund (most defensible). The choice has constitutional and policy implications.

04

Coordinate with Collective Mechanisms and Public Enforcement

In mass harm scenarios, multiple individual punitive awards create structural over-deterrence and multiple punition risk. The preferred architecture: a coordinated redress programme (class/representative action or regulatory settlement) combined with public administrative sanctions — not accumulated individual punitive awards for the same wrongful conduct.

05

In Technology Disputes, Distinguish Disgorgement from Punitive Damages

Where a technology company has extracted value from data or protected works without authorisation, consider whether the objective is to strip the gain (unjust enrichment / disgorgement — a restorative remedy) or to punish the conduct (punitive damages — a sanction requiring proportionality review). Confusing the two will undermine both claims.

Conclusion

The Thesis Confirmed — and Refined

The central argument of the 2010 study survives the stress test of sixteen years and a substantially changed institutional landscape. Punitive damages and unjust enrichment remain distinct in their normative logic, their metric, and the legal standards that govern their application. The former punishes; the latter restores. Conflating them generates systematic distortions that harm both claimants (under-deterrence or overcompensation) and defendants (due process deficits).

What has changed is the terrain on which these distinctions must be drawn. The period 2010–2026 has produced three structural developments that any practitioner in this field must internalise. First, punitive-type mechanisms are no longer alien to civil law systems; they have migrated — but they arrive with conditions attached that the common law tradition did not always require. The Italian, Mexican, and Argentine experiences show that civil law can accommodate deterrent-oriented remedies; they also show that it does so only when legality, foreseeability, proportionality, and quantitative limits are in place. Second, disgorgement has emerged as the most contested remedial instrument of the period — oscillating between restitution and punishment in the United States, while the EU has deliberately excluded it from private enforcement in competition and consumer law. Third, the digital economy has created new forms of causeless enrichment — in particular, the extraction of value from unauthorised data use — that classical unjust enrichment doctrine is well-positioned to address, provided that gain-based restitution is kept analytically separate from punitive damages.

The question for the next decade is not whether civil law can tolerate punitive mechanisms. It is whether, having admitted them, it can maintain the analytical clarity that prevents compensation, disgorgement, and punishment from collapsing into a single, unprincipled instrument.

This matters not only as an academic question but as a practical one: as disputes increasingly cross borders, the characterisation of a remedy as compensatory, restorative, or punitive determines which jurisdiction's law governs, whether recognition and enforcement will be available, and what procedural safeguards apply. Getting the distinctions right is not merely intellectually satisfying — it is strategically essential.
