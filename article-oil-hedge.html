Ecuador's “Oil Insurance”: What It Solves and What It Doesn’t

Hugo Aguiar · March 24, 2026 · 5 min read · Energy Policy

This week, Ecuador's Monetary Policy and Regulation Board issued Resolution JPRFM-2026-009-M, enabling the Central Bank to act as financial intermediary for commodity price hedging operations on behalf of state-owned enterprises. In practice: the institutional path for Petroecuador to contract oil price insurance is nearly clear.

The timing is not coincidental. WTI closed Tuesday at USD 87.27 following the U.S. strike on Iran. A high price is precisely the window to structure an efficient hedge — the price floor you lock in is more favorable the higher the market sits.

What Is Oil Hedging?

A financial mechanism that allows a country or state company to set a minimum sale price for crude through options or derivatives. If the price falls below that floor, the instrument compensates the difference. If it rises, the country doesn't fully capture the upside — that's the cost of the protection. Mexico has done this for over twenty years. In 2009, 2015, and 2020 — the three major oil price collapses — Mexico's “oil insurance” program offset a substantial portion of fiscal revenue losses. It remains the unavoidable reference case.

What Does It Solve for Ecuador?

It reduces fiscal revenue volatility in a dollarized economy with no central bank of its own, where an oil price collapse cannot be absorbed through currency devaluation or monetary expansion. The impact hits the budget directly.

What Doesn’t It Solve?

This is the dimension that public debate in Ecuador consistently overlooks. Hedging protects against drops in a reference price — typically WTI. But Ecuador doesn't sell at WTI. It exports Napo and Oriente, heavy high-sulfur crudes that trade at structural discounts to the international benchmark. That differential — which has historically amounted to losses on the order of USD 200 to 270 million annually — doesn't disappear with a price hedge. If WTI falls and the quality differential widens simultaneously, the instrument covers only part of the problem.

It also doesn't resolve the contractual architecture. The Central Bank resolution is explicit: the BCE acts solely as intermediary, assuming no risk. Costs, premiums, losses, and margin calls fall entirely on Petroecuador. The state pays for the insurance. The relevant question is who structures it, under what conditions, and with what degree of public scrutiny.

The Mexican case is instructive precisely on this point: that program works because it rests on decades of accumulated technical capacity, specialized teams within the Finance Ministry, and established accountability mechanisms. Without that institutional infrastructure, a poorly designed hedge is not insurance — it's a rent transfer toward international financial counterparties with superior information.
